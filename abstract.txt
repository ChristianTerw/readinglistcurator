As telemedicine becomes more routine in outpatient care, clinicians increasingly need to decide whether patients should be seen through telemedicine or traditional face-to-face care; yet, the downstream effect of this choice on subsequent care remains unclear. We applied a T-learner framework to estimate downstream utilization impact (DUI), defined for each patient as the difference between the predicted number of follow-up visits within 3 or 6 months under telemedicine and under face-to-face care. We further decomposed DUI into telemedicine-channel and face-to-face-channel components. Across a Penn Medicine outpatient cohort (N = 160,520), initiating care through telemedicine was associated with approximately 0.19 additional follow-up visits over 3 months and 0.37 over 6 months. DUI was heterogeneous across clinical categories. Mental, behavioral, and neurodevelopmental disorders showed DUI arising mainly through the telemedicine channel, whereas musculoskeletal conditions showed a larger face-to-face escalation component. These patterns were consistent across two follow-up horizons and robust to alternative base learners (LightGBM, Poisson GLM) and to alternative decomposition thresholds. This condition-dependent heterogeneity in the downstream effect of telemedicine is relevant to modality-sensitive triage, channel-specific capacity planning, and payor policy.
